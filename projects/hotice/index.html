Hot Ice

Supplies

  • 4 cups of white vinegar (acetic acid)
  • 4 tablespoons of baking soda (sodium bicarbonate)
  • A pot
  • A glass measuring cup or mason jar (make sure itâ€™s heat safe glass)
  • A dish
  • A spoon

Steps

  1. Pour 4 cups of vinegar into a medium-sized pot.
  2. Add 4 tablespoons of baking soda to the pot. (Note: The key is to add the baking soda slowly so it doesn't bubble over.)
  3. Stir until the baking soda dissolved and stopped fizzing.
  4. Slowly boil the solution over medium-low heat for a little over an hour to remove the extra water.
  5. The solution reduces by about 75% so there will be about 3/4 cup. White powdery crystals will form on the sides of the pot near the top of the solution. (Note: If you boil your solution at a higher temperature it may turn yellow-brownish, but the experiment will still work.)
  6. Pour the concentrated sodium acetate into a glass measuring cup and place it in the fridge to cool. (Note: You can scraped the little bits of dried solution powder off the inside of the pot to use later.)
  7. After about 30-45 minutes, the solution will be cool enough to turn into ice.
  8. Grab the glass dish and place a small pile of the sodium acetate powder from the pot in the center. This will act as a seed for the crystals to start forming.
  9. Carefully take the cooled solution out of the fridge because any bump could start the crystallizing process.
  10. Pour the solution very slowly into the pan and crystals will begin to instantly form.

You can use food coloring to make the ice colorful.

The Science

  The sodium acetate solution in the refrigerator is what is called a supercooled liquid. That means the sodium acetate is in liquid form below its usual melting point. Once you touch, bump, or add a small crystal that is not liquid, crystallization will begin and the liquid will change to a solid. When the molecules in the solute (sodium acetate) are in a solution, they normally are surrounded by a solvent (in this case water molecules).
